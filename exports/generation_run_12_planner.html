Today we are shifting gears from theory and design into the empirical phase of our course. We are talking about planning usability testing. This is a critical juncture in the semester because it directly feeds into your second major assignment, the Usability Design Project. Up until now, you have been analyzing needs and creating prototypes, but without a solid plan for testing, you cannot validate whether those prototypes actually work for humans. In this lecture, we are going to look at how to construct a rigorous test plan, how to write tasks that do not lead the user, and how to recruit participants who actually represent your target audience. Your plan needs to outline the goals of the test, the hypotheses you are investigating, the metrics you will collect, and the equipment you will use. We are looking at both qualitative and quantitative data. Qualitative data tells you why a user struggled, perhaps through their think-aloud commentary, while quantitative data tells you how long it took them or how many errors they made. You need to decide beforehand what success looks like. Are you measuring task completion rates? Are you measuring time on task? Or are you measuring subjective satisfaction using a questionnaire like the System Usability Scale? Another key component of the plan is the consent process. You must ensure ethical standards are met. This means preparing informed consent forms that explain the purpose of the study, how data will be stored, and the participant's right to withdraw at any time without penalty. This is not just bureaucratic red tape. It is about respecting the human beings who are giving you their time and cognitive effort. Without a written plan that covers these bases, you are not conducting science. You are just watching people use software. You need to bring together quantitative metrics and qualitative observations to build a robust argument. The quantitative data gives you the what and the how much. This includes success rates, time on task, error counts, and perhaps system usability scale scores. If eighty percent of users failed to complete the checkout process, that is a hard metric that indicates a major problem. But that number does not tell you why they failed. When a user sighs heavily or says I am completely lost, that is qualitative evidence of frustration. The core concept here is that neither data type is sufficient on its own for a high quality report. You need the metrics to identify the magnitude and frequency of the problem and the observations to understand the root cause. Without this combination, your analysis is shallow. ### 1.0 Introduction
This report details the design, prototyping, and evaluation of
CampusEats, a mobile application designed to streamline food ordering for
university students on campus. The project was undertaken as part of the
IT305 Usability Design Project, addressing the specific user need
identified in the unit scenario: reducing wait times and cognitive load
associated with ordering food during peak university hours. The primary
user group is time-poor students who require a quick, accessible, and
reliable way to browse menus, customize orders, and pay without
navigating complex interfaces or waiting in long physical queues. This
report documents the full usability engineering lifecycle, from initial
design rationale through to evidence-based recommendations for iteration.
The design process was guided by established usability principles and
accessibility standards, with a focus on creating an interactive
prototype that demonstrates key functionality. Subsequent usability
testing with three participants provided both qualitative and
quantitative data, which was analysed to identify critical usability
issues and propose targeted design refinements.
### 2.0 Design Rationale and Accessibility
The design of CampusEats was driven by the core principles of consistency
and feedback, as discussed in Week 5 lecture materials. The goal was to
create an interface that feels immediately familiar to the target user
group, thereby reducing the learning curve and allowing students to
complete their primary task?ordering food?with maximum efficiency.
**2.1 Consistency and Platform Standards**
A key design decision was to adhere strictly to external consistency by
aligning the prototype with Google?s Material Design guidelines. This was
a deliberate choice, as the majority of the target user group uses
Android devices, and leveraging their existing mental models of how
Android applications behave would minimise cognitive friction. For
instance, the primary navigation employs a standard bottom navigation bar
with clearly labelled icons for ?Home?, ?Search?, ?Orders?, and
?Profile?. This pattern is ubiquitous in mobile applications, and placing

it at the bottom of the screen ensures it is reachable with a thumb, a
key ergonomic consideration. Internal consistency was maintained by using
a uniform design language for all interactive elements. All primary
call-to-action buttons, such as ?Add to Cart? or ?Place Order?, are the
same shade of campus-branded teal (#008080) and are consistently placed
at the bottom-right of the screen. This predictability means that once a
user learns the function of this button on one screen, they can transfer
that knowledge to every other screen in the application.
**2.2 Feedback Mechanisms**
Feedback was integrated into every interactive state to ensure the
system?s visibility, a concept central to Nielsen?s heuristics. When a
user taps ?Add to Cart?, a clear animation occurs: the item appears to
fly into the cart icon in the navigation bar, and a subtle haptic pulse
is triggered. This multimodal feedback confirms the action without
requiring the user to navigate away from the menu. For error states, the
design avoids generic alerts. If a user tries to submit an order with an
empty cart, a contextual snackbar appears at the bottom of the screen
with the message, ?Your cart is empty. Add items before placing an
order.? This snackbar automatically dismisses after a few seconds,
providing clear guidance without blocking the user?s flow. A progress
indicator is displayed during payment processing, directly addressing the
user?s need to know the system is working and preventing duplicate
submissions.
**2.3 Accessibility and Inclusive Design**
Accessibility was considered from the initial wireframing stage, not as a
retrofit. The design was evaluated against the four WCAG POUR principles
discussed in Week 8. To ensure the interface is *Perceivable*, all
non-text content, such as food images and icons, includes descriptive
alt-text labels in the prototype?s design annotations. Colour is never
used as the sole means of conveying information; for example, dietary
labels like ?Vegan? or ?GF? (Gluten-Free) use both a coloured tag and a
text label. To make the interface *Operable*, all interactive elements
meet a minimum touch target size of 48x48dp, as recommended by Material
Design, to accommodate users with motor impairments. The entire core
flow, from browsing a menu to placing an order, was designed to be fully
navigable using a keyboard or switch device, with a logical tab order.
For *Understandable*, the language used throughout the interface is plain
and direct, avoiding jargon. Error messages are written to be
constructive, not punitive. Finally, to ensure the design is *Robust*,
the prototype was built using standard Figma components that can be
easily translated into semantic code, ensuring compatibility with screen
readers like TalkBack. A high-contrast mode was also considered, with the
primary text maintaining a contrast ratio of at least 4.5:1 against its
background, verified using a contrast checker plugin.
### 3.0 Usability Test Plan
The usability test was designed to evaluate the efficiency and
learnability of the core ordering flow within the CampusEats prototype.

The plan was structured to gather both quantitative performance data and
qualitative insight into user behaviour and satisfaction.
**3.1 Test Goals and Objectives**
The primary goal was to identify usability obstacles that prevent users
from successfully completing a food order. The specific objectives were
to:
- Measure the task success rate for the primary ?order a meal? flow.
- Identify points of friction or confusion within the navigation and
checkout process.
- Assess user satisfaction with the overall ordering experience.
**3.2 Participant Recruitment**
Three participants were recruited for this study. To move beyond a
convenience sample of peers, a screening questionnaire was used to select
participants who matched the target demographic of university students
but with varying levels of experience with food ordering apps. The final
participant group consisted of:
- **Participant 1 (P1):** A 20-year-old design student who uses food
delivery apps daily.
- **Participant 2 (P2):** A 25-year-old postgraduate engineering student
who rarely uses mobile ordering apps.
- **Participant 3 (P3):** A 22-year-old commerce student who uses apps
weekly and self-identified as having a repetitive strain injury, making
keyboard navigation a preference on their laptop, though they used a
touchscreen for this mobile test.
This mix provided a range of technology proficiency and physical
considerations, aligning with inclusive design testing practices.
**3.3 Tasks and Success Metrics**
Participants were given two scenario-based tasks to complete. The tasks
were designed to be goal-oriented, avoiding step-by-step instructions
that would lead the user.
- **Task 1 (Browsing and Customisation):** ?You?re looking for a healthy
lunch option that is under $12. Find a salad that fits your budget,
customise it to remove an ingredient you don?t like, and add it to your
cart.? The success metric was the ability to add the correctly customised
item to the cart. Time on task was recorded.
- **Task 2 (Checkout and Payment):** ?You?ve decided you?re ready to eat.
Go to your cart and place the order for pickup, paying with a credit
card.? The success metric was reaching the final order confirmation
screen. The number of errors, defined as deviations from the optimal
path, was recorded.
**3.4 Methodology**
The test sessions were conducted in a quiet, private room. Participants
were given an informed consent form to sign, which outlined the study?s
purpose, data handling procedures, and their right to withdraw. A
concurrent think-aloud protocol was employed, where participants were
asked to verbalise their thoughts as they navigated the prototype. The
facilitator used neutral prompts like ?What are you looking at now?? when

participants fell silent, carefully avoiding leading questions. Each
session was screen-recorded, and the facilitator took structured notes on
a log sheet, capturing task completion, time, errors, and critical
incidents.
### 4.0 Analysis of Test Results
The data from the three usability sessions was collated and analysed to
identify patterns. The analysis triangulates quantitative performance
metrics with qualitative observations to uncover the root causes of
usability issues.
**4.1 Task Performance Summary**
The quantitative results revealed a clear usability issue with the
checkout process.
- **Task 1 (Browsing and Customisation):** All three participants
completed this task successfully and efficiently. The average time on
task was 28 seconds. P2, the least experienced user, commented, ?Oh, the
filter is right there, that?s easy,? indicating the menu navigation was
intuitive.
- **Task 2 (Checkout and Payment):** This task presented significant
problems. Only one out of three participants (P1) completed the task
without assistance. P2 failed to locate the checkout button entirely
within the allotted two minutes, and P3 succeeded only after a period of
visible hesitation and exploratory tapping. The average time on task for
the two who completed it was 65 seconds, more than double the time for
Task 1.
**4.2 Qualitative Findings and Triangulation**
The high failure rate on Task 2 was triangulated with the think-aloud
data to identify the root cause. The quantitative data showed *what* was
happening (failure to checkout), and the qualitative data explained *why*.
P2, while staring at the cart screen, said, ?Okay, I see my salad? I want
to pay for it, but I don?t see a ?pay now? or ?next? button. Is it hidden
in a menu?? This was a critical incident. P3 also expressed confusion,
muttering, ?I?m looking for the checkout? maybe it?s up top? No? down
here? Oh, there it is,? before finally tapping the correct button after a
15-second delay. The triangulation of the 66% failure/hesitation rate
with these direct quotes clearly indicates that the ?Place Order?
button?s visual design and placement did not match the users? mental
models. It lacked sufficient visual prominence and was not located where
users expected a primary action button to be. A secondary finding was
that P3, who has a motor impairment, found the small ?remove? icon on the
customisation screen slightly difficult to tap precisely, though they did
not fail the task.
**4.3 Severity Ratings**
Identified issues were rated using a severity scale adapted from
Nielsen?s model, considering both impact and frequency.
- **Issue 1: Checkout Button Discoverability (Severity: 3 - Major).**
This issue prevented two users from completing the primary task

efficiently and caused significant delay for a third. It is a major
problem that must be fixed as a high priority because it directly blocks
the core transaction.
- **Issue 2: Small Touch Target for Customisation (Severity: 2 -
Minor).** This caused slight physical difficulty for one user but did not
lead to task failure. It is a minor usability irritant that should be
addressed to improve the experience for users with motor impairments,
aligning with inclusive design goals.
### 5.0 Recommendations for Design Iterations
Based on the analysis, the following evidence-based recommendations are
proposed for the next design iteration. These are specific and
actionable, directly addressing the usability issues uncovered during
testing.
**5.1 Recommendation for Checkout Button (Addresses Issue 1)**
The primary recommendation is to redesign the cart screen to increase the
discoverability of the checkout action. The current design places a
text-only ?Place Order? button within the cart summary. This should be
replaced with a highly visible, fixed call-to-action button at the bottom
of the screen. The button should be the branded teal colour, fill the
full width of the screen, and include both a clear text label (?Place
Order - $11.50?) and a forward arrow icon. This change leverages the
external consistency of standard e-commerce patterns where the primary
action is a large, sticky button at the bottom. This will ensure the
button is immediately visible and reachable, directly resolving the
mental model mismatch identified by P2 and P3.
**5.2 Recommendation for Customisation Touch Targets (Addresses Issue 2)**
To improve accessibility for users with motor impairments, the touch
target size for the ?remove? and ?add? icons in the customisation flow
should be increased from the current 24x24dp to at least 48x48dp. The
visual icon size can remain the same, but the interactive hit area should
be expanded. This is a simple, high-impact change that adheres to WCAG
Operable guidelines and will prevent accidental taps and physical strain,
as experienced by P3.
**5.3 Recommendation for Post-Iteration Testing**
Following these changes, a second round of usability testing is
recommended, focusing specifically on the checkout flow. This test should
recruit at least one new participant who relies on a screen reader to
further validate the robustness of the design, ensuring the new button is
properly labelled and announced by assistive technology.
### 6.0 Conclusion
This project successfully simulated a professional usability engineering
lifecycle, from applying design principles to an interactive prototype
through to conducting empirical testing and proposing evidence-based
iterations. The initial design for CampusEats was grounded in Material

Design standards and WCAG accessibility principles, resulting in an
intuitive browsing experience. However, the usability testing was crucial
in revealing a significant flaw in the checkout process discoverability
that was not apparent during the design phase. The triangulation of
quantitative failure rates with qualitative user commentary provided a
clear, unbiased diagnosis of the problem. The resulting recommendations
are not subjective opinions but direct responses to observed user
behaviour. This process demonstrates that a user-centred design approach,
even with a small sample size, is invaluable for identifying and
rectifying critical usability barriers before development, ultimately
saving resources and ensuring a more equitable and efficient product for
all students.
